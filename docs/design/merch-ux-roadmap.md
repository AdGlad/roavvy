# Roavvy Merch UX Roadmap

> Companion to: `docs/design/merch-ux-review.md`
> Status: Planning document — not yet scheduled. Prioritise against active backlog.

---

## Prioritisation Framework

Each recommendation is evaluated against:

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| Conversion impact | Likelihood to turn a non-buyer into a buyer |
| Engagement impact | Likelihood to increase repeat opens, shares, design creation |
| Retention impact | Likelihood to increase long-term use and repeat purchases |
| Complexity | Engineering effort (S = 1–3 days, M = 1–2 weeks, L = 3–5 weeks) |

---

## Quick Wins (1–3 days each)

---

### QW-1: Fix "Roavvy Test Tee" in Order History

**What:** Replace the hardcoded product name lookup in `MerchOrdersScreen` that
returns "Roavvy Test Tee" for T-shirt orders. Use the stored title from the
`MerchCartItem` if available, with a fallback of "Travel T-shirt".

**Rationale:** Any user who has previously purchased and returns to check their
order history sees "Roavvy Test Tee". This is the single most trust-damaging
artefact in the product. It takes 10 minutes to fix.

**Complexity:** S

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (eliminates active trust damage for returning users)

**Engagement impact:** MEDIUM (order history is only visited by buyers)

---

### QW-2: Add Price to Option Cards

**What:** Show a "from £X" label on each option card in `PulseMerchOptionScreen`
and `AchievementMerchOptionScreen`. The price is already in `MerchProduct.fromPrice`
and `MerchVariantLookup` — surface it on the card.

**Rationale:** Users are currently selecting designs and investing emotional energy
without knowing the cost. Price revelation at the bottom sheet creates late-funnel
sticker shock. Showing price upfront reduces anxiety and respects the user's decision.

**Complexity:** S

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (removes late-funnel abandonment from price surprise)

**Engagement impact:** LOW

---

### QW-3: Rename "Orders" Tab to "My Collection" in MerchShopScreen

**What:** Change the tab label "Orders" to "My Collection" in `MerchShopScreen`.
Update the empty-state text from "No orders yet. Head to the Shop to order your
first personalised item." to something aspirational: "Your purchased designs will
appear here. Each one tells a story."

**Rationale:** "Orders" is logistics language. "My Collection" is identity language.
This single label change reframes what the tab is for. The user is not tracking
parcels — they are building a personal gallery of travel artefacts.

**Complexity:** S

**Conversion impact:** LOW (does not directly drive purchases)

**Engagement impact:** MEDIUM (sets correct expectation for what the tab is for)

---

### QW-4: Reframe the Confirmation Checkbox

**What:** Rewrite the confirmation checkbox text in `CartItemCheckoutScreen` to be
reassuring rather than legalistic. Current: "I confirm the size, colour, design, and
print positions shown above are correct." Proposed: "I'm happy with my design and
ready to order." Also reduce the `MerchCustomProductWarning` to a single concise
sentence below the checkbox, not above it.

**Rationale:** The current checkbox wording reads as a legal disclaimer and creates
anxiety at the peak emotional moment of the purchase. The user needs reassurance, not
a warning. Moving the no-refund notice below the checkbox reduces its prominence.

**Complexity:** S

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (removes last-stage psychological barrier)

**Engagement impact:** LOW

---

### QW-5: Post-Purchase Screen Shows the Design

**What:** Pass the approved artwork image bytes or the front mockup URL to
`MerchPostPurchaseScreen`. Display the shirt mockup image prominently above the
celebration copy.

**Rationale:** After buying, the first thing a user wants is confirmation that their
design looks right. Currently they see a 🎉 emoji and generic copy. The design image
is the most important piece of information at this moment — and the most shareable asset.

**Complexity:** S (mockup URL is available in the checkout flow; pass it through)

**Conversion impact:** MEDIUM (does not drive first purchase, but reinforces correctness)

**Engagement impact:** HIGH (enables meaningful sharing of the actual design)

---

### QW-6: Reframe the "Share My Order" Action as "Share My Design"

**What:** Change the share button label on `MerchPostPurchaseScreen` from "Share my
order" to "Share my design". Update the share text to include the design title and
country count in a compelling format: "I just had my [title] shirt made — [n]
countries I've visited, designed with Roavvy."

**Rationale:** Sharing "an order" is administrative. Sharing "a design" is personal.
The current share text is generic and does not include the design title generated by
`MerchStory` — the most emotionally compelling piece of copy in the flow.

**Complexity:** S

**Conversion impact:** MEDIUM (organic acquisition from social sharing)

**Engagement impact:** HIGH

---

### QW-7: Rename Customisation Options to Outcome Language

**What:** In `MerchCustomisationSheet`, replace the engineering labels with
outcome-oriented ones:
- "Scatter: Low/Medium/High" → "Arrangement: Structured / Spread / Scattered"
- "Density: Sparse/Balanced/Dense" → "Style: Airy / Balanced / Packed"
- "Layout: Passport/Grid/Landmark" → keep but add visual icons for each layout type
- "Stamps: Entry only / Entry + Exit" → "Stamps: One per country / Entry and exit"

**Rationale:** No one outside engineering knows what "scatter" and "density" mean in
this context. Outcome language helps users choose confidently rather than guessing.

**Complexity:** S

**Conversion impact:** MEDIUM (reduces abandonment from confusing customisation options)

**Engagement impact:** LOW

## Medium Milestones (1–2 weeks each)

---

### MM-1: "Start a Design" Entry Point in the Shop Tab

**What:** Add a CTA to the Shop tab that allows any user to initiate a new design
at any time — without needing a Memory Pulse or Achievement trigger.

Implementation:
- Add a persistent "Design a shirt" or "Create a new design" banner at the top of
  the Cart tab in `MerchShopScreen`.
- Tapping it opens a country selection flow (existing `MerchCountrySelectionScreen`
  or a new guided entry) that leads to `LocalMockupPreviewScreen`.
- Alternatively: add a floating action button to the Shop tab.

The selection screen should be pre-personalised — default to the user's full country
list, with the option to filter by year or continent.

**Rationale:** This is the biggest single structural gap in the current product. There
is no intent-driven entry to merch. Every entry point is passive. This change makes
"I want a shirt" actionable at any time.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** VERY HIGH (unlocks intent-driven purchase path)

**Engagement impact:** HIGH (makes merch accessible without needing a trigger event)

---

### MM-2: Cart Item Gallery View

**What:** Replace the `ListTile`-based cart rendering in `MerchShopScreen` with a
card gallery view. Each saved design shows:
- Full-width or half-width mockup image (using `frontMockupUrl`, or local preview)
- Design story title (from `MerchCartItem.title`)
- Status badge (contextual language, not technical — see QW-3)
- Swipe-to-delete
- Tap to proceed to checkout

**Rationale:** The cart is the holding space for designs the user has already invested
creative effort in. Showing a 56×56 thumbnail alongside "T-shirt · 3 countries" does
not honour that investment. A visual gallery creates the feeling of a curated collection
waiting to be purchased, not a list of pending transactions.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (visual designs in cart remind the user what they liked)

**Engagement impact:** MEDIUM

---

### MM-3: Order History Thumbnails and Story Titles

**What:** Upgrade `MerchOrdersBody` to show:
- Design thumbnail from the order (requires storing the front mockup URL in
  `merch_configs` Firestore document — may already be present for newer orders)
- Story title (the `MerchStory`-generated title, stored at order creation time)
- Travel context line: "42 countries · The Grand Tour"
- Ordered date

If thumbnail is unavailable for older orders, show a styled placeholder with the
country flag emoji list or a map-derived visual.

**Rationale:** Order history should feel like a gallery of things you own — physical
artefacts that tell your travel story. A text-only list with status badges is the
wrong framing entirely.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** MEDIUM (repeat purchase trigger when users revisit their collection)

**Engagement impact:** HIGH (makes the history a feature worth visiting)

---

### MM-4: Streamlined Option Selection — Lead with One Design

**What:** Restructure `PulseMerchOptionScreen` and `AchievementMerchOptionScreen` to
lead with a single full-screen featured design (the `MerchOptionFeaturedCard`, already
exists) and collapse the remaining options behind a "See more styles" disclosure.

- Featured card: full-width, large mockup, prominent "Design This Shirt" CTA
- Below it: a horizontally scrollable strip of 3–4 alternative style thumbnails
- "See all options" link opens the full existing list for users who want to browse

**Rationale:** The current screen presents 15–20 options with equal visual weight. The
"Best Match" featured card exists but the user still sees the full list below it. Most
users cannot process 20 design options — they will defer rather than decide.

Lead with one strong recommendation. Let curious users explore further, but do not
default to overwhelming them.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (reduces choice paralysis — the biggest decision-stage drop-off)

**Engagement impact:** MEDIUM

---

### MM-5: Pre-Purchase Design Sharing

**What:** Add a "Share this design" action to `PulseMerchOptionScreen` option cards
and `MerchOptionFeaturedCard`. Tapping it uses `MerchShareExporter` (already built
and used in `LocalMockupPreviewScreen`) to export the design preview as a share card.

The shared image should include:
- The shirt mockup (front, using the local device render)
- The design title ("The Grand Tour")
- The travel stat ("42 countries")
- A subtle Roavvy wordmark

**Rationale:** The best moment to share is when the user first sees a design that
resonates — before they have spent mental energy on size and colour. Currently nothing
is shareable until after configuration. Moving sharing earlier creates organic marketing
at the point of highest enthusiasm.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** MEDIUM (organic acquisition from social; FOMO from followers)

**Engagement impact:** HIGH (social sharing is a strong engagement signal)

---

### MM-6: "Design from Achievement" Shortcut in Achievements Tab

**What:** Add a direct "Design a shirt" CTA on the achievement detail view for any
achievement that has a `merch` trigger. Currently the `MerchMomentsSection` in Stats
is the only non-Memory Pulse merch entry from achievements. This change puts the
CTA directly on the achievement card the moment it is unlocked.

Also: upgrade the `MerchMomentsSection` product labels from generic names
("Flag Grid Tee", "Passport Stamp Tee") to emotionally framed descriptions
("Passport design", "Flag collection shirt", "Explorer badge tee").

**Rationale:** The achievement unlock is the highest-intent moment for merch discovery.
The path from "I just unlocked Continent Explorer" to "I want a shirt for this" should
be one tap, not three screens of navigation.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (high-intent moments are the best conversion opportunities)

**Engagement impact:** MEDIUM

---

### MM-7: Locked/Unlocked Exclusive Designs

**What:** Show users designs that are "almost unlocked" — designs that become available
when they reach the next milestone. For example, a user at 40 countries sees the
"Half the World" design (50-country milestone) with a lock overlay and the message
"5 more countries to unlock this design."

Implementation:
- Add a "Coming Soon" section below the regular options in `MerchOptionFeaturedCard`
- Show 1–2 near-miss milestone designs that are just ahead of the user's current count
- Lock overlay with progress indicator

**Rationale:** The Strava model: achievements you haven't earned yet create desire to
earn them. Seeing a design you are 8 countries away from is a powerful retention and
re-engagement mechanism. It turns passive travel into goal-driven travel.

**Complexity:** M

**Conversion impact:** MEDIUM (not immediate, but drives long-term re-engagement)

**Engagement impact:** VERY HIGH (creates repeat opens to check progress)

## Major Experience Upgrades

---

### MX-1: Shop Tab Redesign — Personal Discovery Surface

**What:** Redesign the Shop tab from a cart/orders container to a personal merch
discovery surface.

Structure:
1. **Identity header** — travel name, country count, continent count, years of travel
2. **"Ready to design" section** — 2–3 AI-generated design previews personalised to the
   user's most recent achievement or latest travel activity
3. **Collections** — dynamically generated groups:
   - "Your [Year] Collection" (if the current year has travel data)
   - "Europe Series" (if user has 3+ European countries)
   - "Milestone: [Achievement Name]" (most recently unlocked merch achievement)
4. **Saved Designs** — cart items, gallery view
5. **My Collection** — purchased items, thumbnail gallery

The tab becomes a destination worth visiting even without a purchase intent.

**Rationale:** The current Shop tab is an empty cart. A blank page with instructions to
go elsewhere does nothing for engagement or conversion. This redesign turns the tab into
a product that generates purchase desire on its own.

**Complexity:** L

**Conversion impact:** VERY HIGH (creates intent-driven discovery path)

**Engagement impact:** VERY HIGH (shop becomes a feature, not a staging area)

---

### MX-2: "My Travel Collection" — Order History Redesign

**What:** Replace the `MerchOrdersBody` with a full gallery experience.

Features:
- Grid of purchased items, each showing the full shirt mockup
- Design title and travel context
- Tap to expand: full design view, travel story, date purchased, "reorder" and "share"
- Share button generates a shareable image: "My [Title] shirt — [n] countries"
- Empty state: inspiring copy and a design creation CTA ("Start your collection")

**Rationale:** Purchased designs are the physical representation of the user's travel
identity. They deserve to be celebrated and revisited. An order history that looks like
a logistics tracker is a missed opportunity to create emotional connection and drive
repeat purchases.

**Complexity:** L

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (repeat purchase trigger from revisiting the collection)

**Engagement impact:** VERY HIGH (creates a valuable gallery worth returning to)

---

### MX-3: Spotify Wrapped-Style Annual Travel Merch Story

**What:** An annual (or triggered-by-achievement) story screen that presents the user's
year in travel as a narrative, ending with a personalised merch recommendation.

Flow:
- Triggered by: year-end, a significant milestone, or the "Year in Review" feature
- Full-screen animated cards, one at a time:
  - "In 2024 you visited [8] countries"
  - "You crossed [3] continents"
  - "Your longest journey: [Tokyo → London]"
  - "You earned [Continent Explorer]"
  - "Your 2024 shirt:"  → full mockup of the recommended design, with a Buy CTA

**Rationale:** Spotify Wrapped is the most successful social engagement product of the
last decade because it tells a personal story that users want to share. Roavvy has
identical raw material — real travel data — and the merch output at the end of the story
is a natural, earned recommendation.

**Complexity:** L

**Conversion impact:** VERY HIGH (combines emotional storytelling with purchase CTA)

**Engagement impact:** VERY HIGH (highly shareable, viral potential)

---

### MX-4: Wishlist / Saved Designs (Separate from Cart)

**What:** Introduce a distinct concept of "saved designs" — designs the user has
created and liked but is not ready to purchase immediately.

Features:
- "Save for later" button on `LocalMockupPreviewScreen` and option cards
- Saved designs stored locally (or in Firestore) with the country set, template, title
- Accessible from the Shop tab in a "Saved" section
- Designs that have been saved but not purchased for 7 days trigger a push notification:
  "Your [Title] design is waiting"

**Rationale:** Not every user who designs a shirt will buy immediately. Currently, if
they do not complete checkout, the design is lost unless they configured it in the cart
flow. A wishlist captures the intent and enables a re-engagement pathway without losing
the creative work.

**Complexity:** L

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (re-engages users who expressed intent but did not convert)

**Engagement impact:** HIGH (creates a reason to return to the app)

---

### MX-5: Achievement-Locked Merch Drops

**What:** A visible catalogue of exclusive designs that are locked behind achievement
milestones. Users can see what they are working towards.

Features:
- A "Merch Drops" section in the Shop tab (or a dedicated tab)
- Shows all available exclusive designs with lock/unlock status
- Locked designs show the requirement: "Visit [5] more countries to unlock"
- Unlocked designs glow with a gold border and "Unlocked for you" label
- Notification when a new design is unlocked

**Rationale:** Creates a direct link between travel activity and merchandise aspiration.
The user's goal is no longer "I should buy a shirt someday" — it becomes "I want that
50-country design and I'm 8 countries away." This is the most powerful long-term
retention mechanism available without social features.

**Complexity:** L

**Conversion impact:** HIGH (aspirational desire converts to purchase at milestone unlock)

**Engagement impact:** VERY HIGH (travel activity directly drives merch desire)

## Recommended Next 10 Milestones

Ranked by business value, user value, conversion impact, and implementation effort.

---

### M-MERCH-1: Fix "Roavvy Test Tee" + Price Transparency + Post-Purchase Design

**Goal:** Bundle three Quick Wins (QW-1, QW-2, QW-5) that eliminate the biggest trust
and conversion problems in a single milestone.

**Why it matters:** QW-1 removes an active trust-damager. QW-2 removes late-funnel
anxiety. QW-5 creates a shareable post-purchase moment. All three are S-complexity
and mutually independent — bundle them to reduce overhead.

**Complexity:** S (1–2 days combined)

**Expected impact:**
- Eliminates "Roavvy Test Tee" shame for returning users
- Reduces late-funnel price abandonment
- Creates the first post-purchase social sharing moment with the actual design

---

### M-MERCH-2: Reframe Cart, Checkout, and Post-Purchase Copy

**Goal:** Bundle the remaining Quick Wins (QW-3, QW-4, QW-6, QW-7) that reframe
the emotional language throughout the purchase flow.

**Why it matters:** Language matters. "My Collection" vs "Orders". "I'm happy with
my design" vs legal confirmation text. "Share my design" vs "Share my order".
"Spread out" vs "Scatter: High". Each is individually small; combined they transform
the emotional register of the entire flow.

**Complexity:** S (1–2 days combined)

**Expected impact:**
- Reduces last-step checkout abandonment from the confirmation checkbox
- Increases post-purchase social sharing
- Makes the product feel designed with intention, not assembled from defaults

---

### M-MERCH-3: Streamlined Option Selection (Lead with One Design)

**Goal:** Restructure the merch option screens to lead with a single featured design
and hide the rest behind disclosure. Implement MM-4.

**Why it matters:** Choice paralysis at the option selection screen is the biggest
conversion gap between intent and purchase. A user who sees 20 equal options will
defer. A user who sees one great recommendation will buy or explore — both are better
than deferring.

**Complexity:** M (3–5 days)

**Expected impact:**
- Significant reduction in option-screen abandonment
- Faster path from Memory Pulse / Achievement to purchase
- The `MerchOptionFeaturedCard` (already built) becomes the hero, not a header

---

### M-MERCH-4: Intent-Driven Entry — "Design a Shirt" from Shop Tab

**Goal:** Add a "Start a new design" entry point to the Shop tab. Implement MM-1.

**Why it matters:** The single biggest structural gap. Users who arrive with purchase
intent have nowhere to go. This change makes the Shop a shop.

**Complexity:** M (1 week)

**Expected impact:**
- Unlocks an entirely new conversion path: direct intent → design → purchase
- Activates users who are not in a Memory Pulse or Achievement flow
- Increases overall merch revenue by widening the top of the funnel

---

### M-MERCH-5: Cart Gallery View + My Collection Thumbnails

**Goal:** Upgrade cart items to a visual gallery (MM-2) and add thumbnails + story
titles to order history (MM-3). These are closely related and share UI patterns.

**Why it matters:** The cart and order history are the two surfaces that directly
influence repeat purchase decisions. A visual gallery creates emotional recall —
"I remember how good that design looked" — that text lists cannot.

**Complexity:** M (1 week)

**Expected impact:**
- Cart items feel like designs worth buying, not pending items to process
- Order history becomes a gallery that reinforces the decision to purchase again
- "Reorder" action becomes contextually compelling next to the design image

---

### M-MERCH-6: Pre-Purchase Design Sharing

**Goal:** Enable sharing of the design concept from the option selection screen before
the user has configured size and colour. Implement MM-5.

**Why it matters:** The highest-enthusiasm moment in the flow is when the user first
sees a design that captures their travel story. Sharing at this moment creates organic
acquisition. Currently, nothing is shareable at this stage.

**Complexity:** M (3–5 days)

**Expected impact:**
- Organic acquisition from social sharing (design preview as social card)
- Increases social pressure and FOMO for followers who see the shared design
- `MerchShareExporter` already exists — this is primarily a UI and copy task

---

### M-MERCH-7: Achievement CTA Upgrade + Merch Moments Reframe

**Goal:** Add a one-tap "Design a shirt" CTA to achievement detail views (MM-6).
Upgrade `MerchMomentsSection` product labels to emotional descriptions.

**Why it matters:** Achievement unlocks are the highest-intent moments in the product.
The path from unlock to purchase should be one tap. Currently it is three screens.
The Merch Moments section in Stats should feel like a celebration, not a product list.

**Complexity:** M (3–5 days)

**Expected impact:**
- Converts high-intent achievement unlock moments more efficiently
- "Passport collection shirt" converts better than "Passport Stamp Tee"
- More users reach the merch option screen from the achievement flow

---

### M-MERCH-8: Locked/Unlocked Exclusive Designs

**Goal:** Show near-miss exclusive designs to users as aspirational targets. Implement MM-7.

**Why it matters:** This is the highest long-term retention mechanism available within
the merch feature. Seeing the "Half the World" design 8 countries away creates a goal.
Travel apps that create goals create daily opens.

**Complexity:** M (1 week)

**Expected impact:**
- Increases travel scanning activity (users want to unlock designs)
- Creates re-engagement notifications tied to actual travel progress
- Positions Roavvy as a platform, not a transaction

---

### M-MERCH-9: Shop Tab Redesign — Personal Discovery Surface

**Goal:** Redesign the Shop tab as a personalised discovery surface. Implement MX-1.

**Why it matters:** The current Shop tab is an empty cart. This redesign turns it into
the most valuable real estate in the app — a personalised view of the user's travel
identity with immediate design recommendations.

**Complexity:** L (2–3 weeks)

**Expected impact:**
- Shop tab becomes a destination worth visiting on its own
- Every app open has a potential purchase trigger
- Collections (Year / Continent / Achievement) surface contextually relevant designs
  without the user needing to know which template is right for them

---

### M-MERCH-10: Spotify Wrapped-Style Annual Travel Merch Story

**Goal:** Annual (or milestone-triggered) narrative story screen that ends in a merch
recommendation. Implement MX-3.

**Why it matters:** The most powerful merch conversion happens when the user experiences
their travel story as a narrative arc and the shirt feels like the natural conclusion.
This is the experience that makes people say "I want that" rather than "maybe later."

**Complexity:** L (3–4 weeks)

**Expected impact:**
- Highest single-milestone conversion impact
- Highly shareable: each story card is share-worthy even before the purchase CTA
- Differentiates Roavvy from every print-on-demand competitor in the market

## Notes on Sequencing

- M-MERCH-1 and M-MERCH-2 should ship together — they are both S-complexity and
  address trust and language. Ship them as one merged PR.
- M-MERCH-3 and M-MERCH-4 are the highest conversion levers and should be the first
  M-complexity milestones.
- M-MERCH-5 (cart gallery) should come after M-MERCH-4 (entry point) — the gallery
  is more valuable once there is a flow that fills it.
- M-MERCH-9 (shop redesign) should be preceded by M-MERCH-4 — the shop tab entry
  point is a prerequisite for the full redesign.
- M-MERCH-10 (annual story) can be developed in parallel with other milestones as
  it has no shared code dependencies.
